When you plug in your phone or flip on a light switch, you expect the power to flow instantly. But behind your wall outlets sits the largest machine ever built by humanity: the electrical grid. 

Just like our forest fire project, power grids live right at the edge of balance. To stay efficient, they operate near their maximum limits. But this efficiency creates a huge risk. If a single transformer drops offline, its electricity doesn't vanish—it gets pushed to adjacent power lines. If those lines are already full, they trip too, sparking a domino effect known as a **Cascading Blackout**.

### The Rules of the Infrastructure

In this simulation, the power system is modeled by two primary parameters operating over a web of interconnected hubs:
* **Demand Growth ($p$):** As populations run air conditioners and factories operate, electrical load naturally builds up in random areas.
* **Accidental Trip ($f$):** Random real-world accidents (lightning strikes, hardware wear, or fallen trees) will instantly knock a single hub offline.

When a hub's stress color passes **Orange** and hits its maximum threshold, it overloads (**Bright Red**). It instantly dumps its entire electrical volume onto its surrounding neighbors. If the surrounding grid is already heavily strained, you will watch a massive chain reaction tear through the system until it hits a pocket of the grid with empty capacity to absorb the shock.

### What This Teaches Us About Real Blackouts
This simulation mirrors the famous **Northeast Blackout of 2003**, where a single power line hitting a tree branch in Ohio caused a massive cascading shutdown that eventually cut power to over 50 million people across North America. It demonstrates that you don't need a massive system-wide attack to crash a grid; you just need a normal system running too close to its critical threshold.
